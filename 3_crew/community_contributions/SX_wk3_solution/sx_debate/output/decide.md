**The con‑side (“It is better not to be an entrepreneur than an employee”) is more convincing.**

**Why this side prevails**

| Criterion | Pro‑entrepreneurship argument | Con‑entrepreneurship argument | Evaluation |
|-----------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|------------|
| **Scope of evidence** | Cites a single study showing average salaries for “entrepreneurs who raised funding” ($211k vs $59k). | Cites two widely‑cited statistics: ~20 % of startups fail in year 1, ~50 % fail in five years. | Con side presents failure‑rate data that directly addresses the most critical risk of entrepreneurship; the pro side’s salary data is limited to a specific subset (funded entrepreneurs) and does not speak to the average experience of most entrepreneurs. |
| **Addressing counter‑arguments** | Acknowledges risk only superficially (“hard work, resilience”), then immediately asserts that rewards outweigh the challenges. | Recognizes the appeal of autonomy and potential higher earnings, but then systematically lists job‑security deficits, financial risk, time demands, burnout, and work‑life imbalance. | Con side explicitly preempts the most common objections to entrepreneurship, making its case more robust. |
| **Logical consistency** | Claims “greater job security” for entrepreneurs because they “have more control.” This is a logical fallacy: control does not eliminate market volatility or personal financial risk. | Highlights that lack of salary, benefits, and predictable income is a fundamental disadvantage for most people. | The pro side’s central claim is logically flawed; the con side’s claims align with reality. |
| **Relevance to the motion** | Presents entrepreneurship as universally “better” for everyone. | States that entrepreneurship is *not* for everyone, implying that the employee route can be better for a large portion of the population. | The motion asks whether entrepreneurship is *better* overall; a balanced view that identifies a majority preference for employment makes a stronger stance. |
| **Risk–reward balance** | Emphasizes higher earnings and legacy but ignores the high probability of failure and the potential loss of livelihood. | Emphasizes that the probability of failure, high effort, and burnout often outweigh the potential rewards for most. | The con side more convincingly balances risk and reward, whereas the pro side over‑estimates rewards relative to risk. |

**Conclusion**

While the pro‑side paints an appealing picture of autonomy, higher income, and legacy, it under‑states and even mischaracterizes the principal risks—particularly the high failure rates and lack of financial stability. The con‑side, by contrast, presents a fuller, more realistic appraisal that acknowledges some entrepreneurial benefits but foregrounds the significant disadvantages that affect most people. Consequently, the argument that **“It is better not to be an entrepreneur than an employee”** is more persuasive based on the evidence and reasoning offered.
