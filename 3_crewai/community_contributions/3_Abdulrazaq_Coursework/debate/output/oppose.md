I oppose the motion.

**Strict laws to regulate LLMs would do more harm than good because they would freeze a fast-moving technology with broad public benefits while failing to stop bad actors.** The core problem is not that LLMs exist; it is how people use them. The same model that can generate misinformation can also improve education, accessibility, coding, translation, and healthcare support. Heavy-handed laws would punish legitimate users, burden startups, and entrench a few large firms that can afford compliance.

There is also a practical issue: **strict regulation lags behind technology**. By the time lawmakers define what counts as an LLM, the technology will have changed. Overly rigid rules become obsolete, vague rules become arbitrary, and either way they create uncertainty. The result is not safety—it is bureaucracy, delays, and a chilling effect on innovation.

Most importantly, **strict laws create a false sense of security**. Harmful content, fraud, and manipulation do not disappear because a law says they should. Malicious actors will ignore restrictions, while compliant developers carry the cost. That means regulation often hits the honest players hardest and leaves the real threat intact.

A better approach is targeted, flexible safeguards: enforce existing laws against fraud and privacy violations, require transparency where appropriate, and focus oversight on high-risk uses rather than the technology itself. **We should regulate misuse, not smother the tool.**

So the opposition is clear: strict laws on LLMs would be blunt, outdated, and counterproductive. **Responsible innovation needs smart guardrails, not heavy shackles.**
