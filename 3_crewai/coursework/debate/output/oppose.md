I oppose the motion because **strict laws to regulate LLMs would do more harm than good**.

First, **LLMs are a general-purpose technology, not a single dangerous product**. Regulating them heavily would be like imposing special laws on calculators, search engines, or word processors just because some people might misuse them. The real issue is misuse, and misuse should be handled through existing laws against fraud, harassment, defamation, and identity theft—not by strangling an entire technology class.

Second, **strict regulation would crush innovation and concentrate power**. The burden of compliance would fall hardest on startups, researchers, and smaller companies, while giant corporations could afford the lawyers and audits. That means the result of “safety” would often be the opposite: fewer competitors, less open research, and more control in the hands of a few dominant firms. Overregulation does not create accountability; it creates monopolies.

Third, **LLMs change too quickly for rigid laws to work well**. By the time lawmakers define requirements, the models, uses, and risks will already have evolved. Strict legal rules would quickly become outdated, vague, or overbroad, punishing beneficial uses like education, accessibility, coding assistance, and medical support. In practice, rigid laws would slow down helpful applications faster than harmful ones.

Finally, **the better solution is targeted enforcement and flexible standards**. Use consumer protection law, copyright law, data privacy rules, and anti-fraud enforcement where they already fit. Encourage transparency, testing, and voluntary best practices without turning innovation into a legal minefield.

So the choice is not between “regulation” and “no regulation.” It is between **smart, targeted oversight** and **heavy-handed laws that stifle progress, entrench big players, and punish beneficial innovation**. For that reason, I strongly oppose strict laws regulating LLMs.
